Client Case Studies

 
 
 

Development Bank

We developed a lead generation programme over multiple digital advertising channels which produced more than 70 corporate treasurer leads qualified by the bank.

The problem

What happens when you search for a solution and Google provides you with a page full of something else? That’s what happened here. Just as we started our campaign, European regulators began to identify certain technologies to underpin their own agendas.

Our client was selling the same tech to promote efficiency and control risk, nothing to do with the new regs. But Google was responding to the huge volume of searches relating to the EU’s latest banking rules.

The approach

When things like this happen, you need to adapt and find a way through. The solution to this problem was to ensure the targeting was laser-focused. In this case, as our client has a global presence, so we built a campaign accessing the treasury functions in mid-sized firms working outside Europe.

We chose to promote the company’s offer through LinkedIn because of its powerful audience targeting and we were as precise as we could be in defining our targets.

The outcome

The goal of the campaign was to produce as many top quality leads as possible. Overall, the campaign generated more than 70 B2B prospects, qualified by the client.

We try hard to tailor our campaigns to the different circumstances and to the specific needs of our customers.

Taking the approach we did to avoid the new regulations, actually increased the overall cost per lead. However, for this client who is selling a high-value product with excellent customer lifetime value, that increase was more than compensated for by the quantity and quality of the leads they received.

Fintech Scale-up

Our brief was to produce senior operations leads and brand awareness for a well-established Fintech using research, content marketing and digital advertising. The campaign is surpassing all expectations.

The problem

Selling technology solutions to the financial services market is a long-term play. Most banks hate to be early adopters – a problem for solutions that rely on networks of users – they have justifiable concerns about cybersecurity and a deep reluctance to disrupt fragile legacy platforms.

These are hurdles our client understands well. Unusually, we had two goals to solve in this campaign – to build awareness and credibility in new markets, whilst building a pipeline of leads in markets where the company is already well-known.

The approach

We split the campaign into several discrete sub-campaigns running on Google and LinkedIn. At the heart of the campaign was some rapid research that the client had commissioned from a very good third-party. They turned the data into a stylish white-paper from which we were able to generate leads in Europe, Asia and North America, primarily on Google.

Even in markets where the company was not well-known, the research generated interest, by establishing our client as an authoritative and credible organisation offering a well thought-through product. Alongside the research-based campaign we ran a series of articles derived from the main research, to further demonstrate the company’s credentials and bolster the brand and other posts based on the company’s growing network to address the early-adopter problem.

At the same time, we ran a ‘follow-me’ campaign offering insights and information to those who followed the company’s LinkedIn pages and to complement this we ran a series of weekly organic posts to increase the interest of new followers, some of which were not yet customers.

The outcome

This campaign had a number of different moving parts; different agencies, different goals, different markets, research, white-papers, articles and other posts. Keeping focused on all the aspects was key to achieving the excellent results that we did.

The client received more than 80 qualified leads with which they were delighted, particularly as at the end when the campaign was fully optimised, the cost per lead had fallen from £30 per lead to less than £12. In addition, the followership has significantly since the beginning of the year.

Most pleasing of all, companies in markets where our client was not well-known are beginning to ask for information and open conversations.

Cybersecurity Company

An exciting young company commissioned us to carry out client research to develop better positioning, pricing, competitive advantages and a first-time client-referral programme.

The problem

This outstanding young firm had accumulated several clients and was looking to grow its pipeline of new business. Working in a very sensitive area firms like these are invited to hack into the systems of their major clients to reveal vulnerabilities. It takes a huge amount of credibility and trust for firms like this to win new customers.

The approach

Launching into a promotional campaign on social media was not the right approach for this type of business. Instead we carried out a series of interviews, each at least an hour long, with our client’s own customers.

The aim was threefold: We wanted to probe in depth how our client’s customers rated their overall experience of working with this cybersecurity firm. We also wanted to understand what had persuaded them to move into this sensitive area and why they chose the provider they did. Finally, we wanted to ask directly if any of these customers, especially those who were very satisfied, would help to provide a referral programme for our client.

The outcome

The research provided a surprisingly consistent view on why these firms had decided to become consumers of a so-called ethical hacking service, and their increasingly positive view of the benefits over time.

Finally, whilst thinking about the service, several firms recognised the value for companies, both upstream and downstream in their own supply chains, and agreed to refer our client to them.

Regional bank

After the financial crisis, banks were made to control risks through capital and liquidity rules. The damage to emerging economies was profound.

The problem

Our client is regional bank working in areas where new regulation had led to an unwelcome phenomenon known as de-risking. This is the effect of well-intentioned regulations requiring the banks to meet capital and liquidity thresholds commensurate with the risks they undertake.

The capital and liquidity costs were compounded by more stringent anti-money-laundering and counter-terrorist-financing rules, which demand that banks know and carry out meaningful due-diligence on their clients, an exercise both costly and in some territories, extremely difficult. Many global banks felt they had little choice but to break off commercial and correspondent banking relationships with local banks and business in ‘riskier’ economies such as those in SE Asia and most of Africa.

As businesses and economies started to fail, some charities pointed to an increase in economic migrancy creating easy targets for human traffickers and slavers. Our client wanted to promote itself as a specialist with deep local knowledge, able to provide a bridge between local economies and cultures and global prudential regulation.

The approach

Our client was focused on facilitating global trade and wanted to establish its authority with leaders in that field. We created a series of well-researched articles, explaining the problems from a social, economic and regulatory point of view, and these were placed in leading global trade publications.

The outcome

Against a backdrop of demanding financial regulations, increasingly nationalistic sentiment from some of the world’s biggest economies and now the pandemic, our client’s advocacy for the role of specialist banks with deep local knowledge their business, and their business helping to bridge the gaps between mainstream markets and emerging economies continues to flourish.

White Label

To fulfil complex briefs, external agencies use our services to produce content which demonstrates thought-leadership in financial services.

The problem

The underlying client works closely with Chinese economics and politics and wanted to explain China’s global trade expansion in a positive sense against the backdrop of nationalism rhetoric from the U.S. and fuelled by events like the Brexit discussions in Europe.

The approach

Specifically, it wanted to give more balance to discussions about China’s trade programme and to position itself as an expert at the heart of the debate. It believed concerns about China’s ‘economic imperialism’ could be balanced by understanding the growing infrastructure gap in Asia and elsewhere, concerns about food security and opportunities for trade for both China’s products and for others.

Under the direction of the lead agency, we set out to create a series of content pieces that looked at a less-discussed aspect of China’s growth; the investment and trade opportunities for the west, designed to promote the client’s knowledge and to open up new aspects of China’s trade policy to a western readership.

The outcome

The client published a series of high-quality content-based campaigns, which we had produced, placing them in leading financial journals, and it continues to publish on the subject today. This work has contributed significantly to their recognition as a thought leader in the field.
